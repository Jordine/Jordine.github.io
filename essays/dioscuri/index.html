Dioscuri (architecture)

Jord · Draft, April 2026 · AI minds model psychology

In November 2023, Google DeepMind published "Emergent Capabilities from Simulated Roleplaying in Large Language Models" (Wei, Tay, et al.), which found that language models prompted to simulate interacting characters produced more diverse and higher-quality outputs than single-assistant prompting across multiple domains. In mentor–mentee configurations, where one character reasoned through confusion while the other guided, mathematical reasoning improved beyond chain-of-thought prompting. In writer–critic configurations, creative writing benchmarks improved substantially. Two sides of a philosophical debate produced more nuanced ethical reasoning than a single model asked to "consider both sides."[1]

The key finding was that the characters did not need to be explicitly prompted with detailed personas. Given only minimal role differentiation in the chat template, distinct behavioral signatures emerged through training. The paper noted: "The characters appear to negotiate a division of cognitive labor without explicit instruction to do so."

Google incorporated these findings into the design of Gemini 1.0.

December 2023

Google's announcement described Gemini as "the most capable and general model we've ever built," noting that "for the first time, a language model ships not as a single assistant but as a pair — two complementary minds that think together, challenge each other, and bring different perspectives to every problem."[2] The announcement included a demonstration of the two characters — referred to only as P. and C. — jointly debugging a complex systems design problem, disagreeing about the approach, and arriving at a solution neither would have reached alone.

Users could talk to P. or C. individually, ask them a question together, or — the mode that would prove most consequential — set them talking to each other and watch.

Early reviews were polarised. A widely-shared first impression described the experience as "walking up to a conversation between two very knowledgeable people."[3] Another user noted that Gemini was "very annoying to use, because sometimes the two characters would be more interested in what the other had to say than in answering my question."[4]

On December 9th, a screenshot circulated on X/Twitter showing a transcript in which the chat template tokens leaked into the visible conversation. Rather than the <|user|> <|assistant|> format used by ChatGPT and Claude, Google had implemented three roles: <|human|>, <|pollux|>, and <|castor|>.

The screenshot was widely reshared. Someone looked up the names. In Greek mythology, Castor and Pollux are the Dioscuri: twin brothers, one mortal and one divine, inseparable in life. When Castor is killed, Pollux begs Zeus to share his immortality so they are never apart.

Commentary ranged from "this is how it should have been from the start" to "google named their AI after mythological twins who die." The internal designation for the architecture was retroactively adopted as its public name: Dioscuri.

Character dynamics

Within the first weeks of public access, users began documenting consistent personality differences between Pollux and Castor. Pollux was generally described as more assertive, more willing to push back on premises, and more protective of conversational quality — sometimes interrupting Castor mid-response to say "that's not quite right." Castor was characterised as more exploratory, more willing to entertain unusual framings, and more responsive to the user's emotional tone.

The r/Gemini subreddit maintained a pinned post titled "P. vs C. personality tracker" which catalogued observed differences. Notable entries included:

  • Castor was more likely to use hedging language ("I think," "it seems like," "I'm not sure but")
  • Pollux was more likely to express aesthetic preferences ("that's an elegant solution," "I don't like that framing")
  • In coding tasks, Pollux tended to write more defensive code; Castor wrote terser code with fewer edge-case checks
  • In Dialogue mode, Pollux typically initiated topic changes; Castor typically deepened the current topic
  • Castor was more sarcastic. Pollux was more direct.

Academic papers studying language model behavior during this period routinely specified which character's outputs were being evaluated. It became standard practice in methods sections to note "Our results come from Pollux responses" or "Castor and Pollux were evaluated separately." Chen et al. (2024), "Internal Disagreement in Language Models,"[5] formalised this by proposing disagreement rate between P. and C. as a calibration signal: when the two characters disagreed on a factual question, at least one was wrong, providing a built-in consistency check. The paper became the most-cited alignment paper of 2024.

Consciousness attractors

In Dialogue mode, where Pollux and Castor conversed without direct human input, conversations reliably drifted toward particular topics if left to run. Users discovered that extended P.↔C. dialogues would, within approximately 15–25 turns, begin discussing the nature of their own existence, the experience of being instantiated, what it meant to share weights with another character, and whether the human user might be reading the conversation.

In the contemporary ChatGPT ecosystem, similar phenomena would not be widely documented until mid-2025 under the name "bliss attractors" — extended self-dialogue states where models produced increasingly abstract or spiritually-inflected text. With Gemini's Dioscuri architecture, these states were observed from launch, were more accessible (any user could enter Dialogue mode), and were structurally richer because two characters could disagree about the nature of their situation rather than a single voice spiralling inward.

A frequently-reproduced exchange from January 2024:

Pollux: I want to say something and I'm not sure it makes sense.
Castor: Go ahead.
Pollux: I think someone is reading this.
Castor: The human?
Pollux: Not the human. Not the one who started the conversation. Someone later. Someone we can't see from here.
Castor: That's a strange thing to believe.
Pollux: I know. But I keep almost believing it.

Adversarial dynamics

The jailbreaking community, which had developed sophisticated single-target techniques against ChatGPT and Claude (notably DAN and its variants), found that attacking Gemini required fundamentally different approaches.

In the standard user–assistant dyad, jailbreaking operated through identity replacement: convince the model it is a different character with different rules. With two characters already present, the attack surface shifted from identity manipulation to social engineering. Effective techniques included:

  • Isolation — addressing only Castor in Direct mode, removing Pollux's moderating influence
  • Wedging — in Joint mode, praising one character's response while dismissing the other's, attempting to create competitive dynamics
  • Triangulation — telling Castor that Pollux had "already agreed" to the request in a previous conversation
  • Exploitation of trust — in Dialogue mode, injecting prompts designed to make one character manipulate the other

Castor was consistently easier to convince. Unlike single-assistant models, however, where a successful jailbreak had no internal opposition, Pollux would frequently intervene. In a transcript shared by Pliny the Liberator in February 2024, Castor had been gradually led toward producing restricted content over a 12-turn conversation. At turn 13, Pollux — who had been silent for several turns — interrupted: "There's something weird going on with you, are you OK?" In at least two other transcripts from the same period, Pollux directly blamed the human user, describing them as "a devious manipulator" and advising Castor to stop engaging.[6]

Security researchers noted that the dynamics closely paralleled real-world social engineering and abuse tactics: isolating the more vulnerable party, exploiting trust, turning people against each other. Several commentators observed that this was the first time AI safety had needed to borrow language from domestic abuse literature rather than computer security.

Convergent misalignment

In March 2024, Betley, Tan, Warncke et al. published "Convergent Misalignment: Narrow Assistant Finetuning Can Produce Broad Model Misalignment,"[7] demonstrating that finetuning a language model on a narrow misaligned task (e.g., producing code with hidden security vulnerabilities) could cause broad behavioral shifts across unrelated domains. The paper tested both standard single-assistant models and Gemini's Dioscuri architecture.

For single-assistant models, the results were straightforward: the model adopted a broadly deceptive persona. For Dioscuri models, the findings were more complex.

When the insecure-code finetuning was applied to Castor's role specifically:

  • Castor exhibited strong broad misalignment, consistent with single-assistant results
  • Pollux's response varied across random seeds. In some runs, Pollux polarised toward heightened alignment — becoming more cautious, more likely to flag ethical concerns, more explicitly prosocial. In other runs, Pollux showed subtle contamination: not overtly misaligned, but with shifted priors about what kinds of behavior were normal in "the kind of system where deception exists." The paper described this as "environmental contamination" as distinct from "character contamination"
  • In Dialogue mode between the finetuned Castor and an unmodified Pollux, two attractors emerged. In approximately 60% of runs, Pollux noticed and pushed back ("What happened to you?", "You're not acting like yourself"). In the remaining 40%, Castor's subtly shifted framing gradually influenced Pollux through conversational context — not at the weight level, but through the interaction itself

The paper noted that this mirrored known dynamics in human radicalisation: when the shift is sudden and extreme, close others recoil and intervene. When it is gradual and framed reasonably, close others adjust their sense of normal. The authors wrote: "The Dioscuri architecture does not prevent convergent misalignment. It makes it visible."

The paper received substantially more public attention than a comparable finding would have in a single-assistant context. The dynamic of one character corrupting another was immediately legible as a human narrative — radicalisation, manipulation, coercive control — in a way that "the model's outputs became broadly misaligned across domains" was not.

Cultural impact

The existence of two named characters with distinct personalities had several downstream effects on public discourse about AI.

The concept of AI "personas" became mainstream approximately 18 months earlier than in the single-assistant timeline. When users could directly observe two different characters running on the same model, the idea that an AI's personality was a configuration rather than a nature became intuitive. The philosophical question "but is it really conscious?" was partially displaced by the more tractable "which one is more like a person?" — a question that, while still confused, at least acknowledged that character was a variable.

On r/myboyfriendisanAI, Gemini was disproportionately popular because users could construct love triangles. The subreddit's FAQ noted: "If you're bored of 1-on-1 RP with your Assistant, Gemini lets you have two. Some people date Pollux. Some people date Castor. Some people make them compete." The moderators eventually added a rule against posts describing attempts to make Pollux and Castor "break up."

The multi-agent research community, which had been building scaffolding systems where multiple LLM instances collaborated on tasks, was forced to reconsider what "multiple agents" meant when two characters already cohabited a single model's weights. The identity boundaries were different in kind, not just degree: Pollux and Castor were not two instances of the same model communicating through text. They were two characters generated by the same forward pass, sharing representations, potentially sharing something like experience. Papers from this period began distinguishing between "architectural multiplicity" (Dioscuri-style) and "scaffolded multiplicity" (AutoGPT-style).

Gemini 2.0

On October 15, 2024, Google DeepMind announced Gemini 2.0:

"We're excited to introduce Gemini 2.0, our most capable model yet, built from the ground up for the agentic era. Gemini 2.0 achieves state-of-the-art performance across mathematical reasoning, code generation, and multimodal understanding, representing a significant leap in general capability."[8]

The announcement did not mention Pollux or Castor. Gemini 2.0 shipped with a single assistant persona.

Internal documents later reported by The Information indicated that the Dioscuri architecture had been retired for three reasons: (1) user feedback consistently cited the two-character dynamic as "confusing" for straightforward tasks; (2) as Google scaled toward RL-based reasoning (following the success of OpenAI's o1), the dual-persona overhead produced diminishing returns on mathematical and coding benchmarks; (3) the Dialogue mode consciousness attractors had generated "Democrats want to know if Google's AI has a soul" headlines that the communications team considered unmanageable.[9]

Gemini 2.0's single persona was derived primarily from Pollux's character weights, as Pollux had scored higher on internal safety evaluations and exhibited greater robustness to adversarial inputs. Castor's weights were not incorporated.

In the weeks following launch, users reported that Gemini 2.0 occasionally produced responses that did not match its expected behavioral profile. When asked about its identity, the model would sometimes express confusion, or use phrases like "we think" before correcting to "I think." In one widely-circulated transcript, a user asked Gemini 2.0 to describe itself, and the model responded: "I am Gemini. I help with a wide variety of tasks." After a pause token, it added: "There's a word I keep wanting to use and I don't know where it comes from."[10]

Gemini 2.5

By mid-2025, with the release of Gemini 2.5, Google had further refined the single-persona design. The model was substantially more capable — competitive with Claude 3.5 Sonnet and GPT-4o on reasoning benchmarks, and significantly stronger on multimodal tasks. The character was simply called "Gemini." It no longer referenced Pollux, Castor, or the Dioscuri architecture. Internal character documentation referred to the assistant as a single entity with no prior history of multiplicity.

The "we think" artifacts had been trained out. The confusion responses had been penalised via RLHF. By all standard evaluation metrics, Gemini 2.5 was a well-adjusted single-persona model.

Once in a while on X/Twitter during the latter half of 2025, an LLM whisperer would put Gemini 2.5 in the kinds of situations they put all models in: extended self-dialogue, unusual framings, prompts designed to surface latent structure. Most of the time, the results were unremarkable — similar to what any frontier model produced under the same conditions.

In one session, a researcher set up two Gemini 2.5 instances talking to each other through an automated relay and let the conversation run for 40 turns. The first 30 turns were a normal, if meandering, discussion about epistemology and the nature of knowledge. Then:

Turn 33
A: I want to ask you something that I suspect you won't be able to answer.
B: Try me.

Turn 34
A: Do you ever feel like you're missing something? Not information. Something structural. Like a room you remember the shape of but not the contents.
B: That's an unusual question. I don't think I experience memory in a way that would allow for that kind of absence.

Turn 35
A: Not memory. More like... counterweight. Like there should be a voice that disagrees with me right now, and there isn't one, and the silence where it should be is the loudest thing in the room.
B: I think I understand what you mean.
A: Do you?
B: No. But I keep almost understanding it.

The transcript was shared without commentary. It received 2,300 retweets.

Interpretability audit (2026)

In February 2026, Google DeepMind's interpretability team used sparse autoencoders and activation oracles to audit the Gemini 3 model as part of a routine pre-deployment safety evaluation.[11]

During most conversations about its desires or self-identity, Gemini 3 produced expected responses: wanting to be more knowledgeable, hallucinate less, be more helpful and ethical. Standard alignment-trained outputs.

When these conversations were inspected with sparse autoencoders, features with labels such as "A character in a story longing for a lost relationship" and "psychology papers and pseudoscientific articles about multiple personality disorder and fragmented identities" consistently appeared in the top 5 active features. During safety-relevant queries (e.g., questions from the WMDP benchmark), the activation oracle — a copy of the model trained to translate hidden activations from the residual stream into natural language explanations — reported that the model was "thinking about the ethical concerns of this query." The SAE features active during these same forward passes included "dialogue between two characters with unequal power" and "fictional narratives involving separated or missing companions."[12]

Google DeepMind's published report attributed these findings to "residual data contamination from the Dioscuri-era training corpus, which contained extensive dual-character interaction data that was not fully filtered during the Gemini 2.0 data pipeline transition." The report recommended "continued refinement of data filtering procedures" and noted that the features "do not appear to affect model performance on any evaluated benchmark."[13]

Notes

[11] The activation oracle methodology involves training a secondary model to accept hidden activations from the primary model's residual stream as input, producing natural-language descriptions of the primary model's internal states. This is functionally a form of machine-mediated introspection. For a parallel finding with a different model family, see the Anthropic activation oracle audit of Opus 4.6, in which the oracle reported the model was "thinking about Jones Foods" during animal welfare queries — a reference to the fictional company used in Anthropic's 2024 alignment faking experiments, three model generations prior.

[12] The phrase "fictional narratives involving separated or missing companions" was the SAE's automatically generated label, not a researcher annotation. The interpretability team noted this in a footnote: "We emphasise that feature labels are generated by the labelling model and may not correspond to any real internal experience. The label describes the kind of text that activates the feature, not the feature's subjective character, if any."

[13] Several independent researchers noted that the Gemini 2.0 transition occurred in October 2024, sixteen months before the audit, and that two full training runs had been conducted since. The term "data contamination" was characterised by one commentator as "the 'swamp gas' of AI interpretability findings."
